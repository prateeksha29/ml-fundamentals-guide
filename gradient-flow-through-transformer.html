You know how to derive the gradient for individual components — sigmoid, softmax, BatchNorm, dropout. You know the transformer architecture — attention, FFN, residuals, LayerNorm. This article connects the two: how do gradients actually flow through a complete transformer block during training?

Understanding this gradient flow is what separates "I can implement a transformer" from "I understand why transformers train stably at 96 layers." Every section here answers a concrete interview question.

01 — The Gradient HighwayGradient Through Residual Connections

Every sub-layer in a transformer has a residual connection: the input is added to the sub-layer's output. This seemingly trivial addition is the single most important design choice for training deep networks.

The math

For a residual block $y = x + f(x)$, where $f$ is the sub-layer (attention or FFN), the gradient is:

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = I + \frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}$$

The identity matrix $I$ means the gradient is always at least 1 in every direction. Even if $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ vanishes completely — the sub-layer contributes nothing — the gradient still flows through the skip connection undiminished.

What this means for deep networks

In a network with $L$ residual layers, the gradient from the loss to the input passes through $L$ such terms. Without residuals, the gradient is a product: $\prod_{l=1}^{L} \frac{\partial f_l}{\partial x_l}$. If each factor is less than 1, the product shrinks exponentially — vanishing gradients. With residuals, the gradient includes a sum of paths that skip layers entirely:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_0} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L} \cdot \prod_{l=1}^{L}\left(I + \frac{\partial f_l}{\partial x_l}\right)$$

When you expand this product, you get $2^L$ terms — one for every possible combination of "go through the sub-layer" or "skip it" at each layer. Crucially, one of those terms is just $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L}$ with no sub-layer transformations at all — a direct gradient highway from the loss to the input. This is why GPT-3's 96 layers can train while a 96-layer vanilla network cannot.

Without residuals (left), gradients are products that shrink exponentially. With residuals (right), a direct gradient highway bypasses all sub-layers. Arrow thickness represents gradient magnitude.

02 — Re-normalizing GradientsGradient Through LayerNorm

You derived the BatchNorm backward pass in the backpropagation article. LayerNorm's backward is the same formula, but applied over the feature dimension instead of the batch dimension.

For input $x$ with $d$ features, the forward pass is $\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}}$, then $y_i = \gamma \hat{x}_i + \beta$. The backward pass for the input gradient, letting $d\hat{x}_i = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y_i} \cdot \gamma$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_i} = \frac{1}{d\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} \left(d \cdot d\hat{x}_i - \sum_j d\hat{x}_j - \hat{x}_i \sum_j d\hat{x}_j \hat{x}_j \right)$$

This formula has three terms: the direct gradient, minus the mean gradient, minus the variance-correlated gradient — identical structure to BatchNorm. The key difference is that the sums are over features (dimension $d$) rather than over the batch.

What LayerNorm does to gradients

LayerNorm doesn't just normalize activations in the forward pass — it also re-normalizes gradients in the backward pass. If you sum the output gradients $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_i}$ over the feature dimension, the mean-correction term ensures they sum to zero. This prevents any one feature from dominating the gradient signal.

Intuition
LayerNorm acts as a gradient stabilizer at every layer boundary. Forward pass: activations are centered and scaled. Backward pass: gradients are centered and scaled by the same mechanism. Neither signal can drift or explode as it passes through the network.

03 — The Heart of the ModelGradient Through Attention

The attention mechanism computes $\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$. Backprop must flow through three stages: the value weighting, the softmax, and the score computation.

Stage 1: Through the value weighting

The output is $O = AV$ where $A$ is the attention weight matrix. Given upstream gradient $dO$:

$$dA = dO \cdot V^T \qquad dV = A^T \cdot dO$$

These are standard matrix multiplication gradients. The key insight: $dV = A^T \cdot dO$ means a value vector receives gradient in proportion to how much attention it received. If position $j$ was barely attended to ($A_{ij} \approx 0$), its value vector gets nearly zero gradient — it learns nothing from this interaction.

Stage 2: Through softmax

The softmax Jacobian for row $i$ is $\frac{\partial A_{ij}}{\partial S_{ik}} = A_{ij}(\delta_{jk} - A_{ik})$, where $S = \frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}$ is the score matrix. Given $dA$ from stage 1, the gradient through softmax for each row is:

$$dS_{i} = A_i \odot \left(dA_i - \sum_j dA_{ij} \cdot A_{ij}\right)$$

where $\odot$ is element-wise multiplication. This is the same softmax backward you derived for cross-entropy, but applied to each row of the attention matrix independently.

Attention collapse — the gradient problem
When one attention weight dominates (say $A_{i,3} \approx 1$ and all others $\approx 0$), the softmax Jacobian becomes nearly zero for all non-dominant positions. The gradient for those positions vanishes — the model can't learn to attend to them. This is attention collapse: the rich get richer (the dominant position keeps its gradient) while all other positions are frozen out. Multi-head attention mitigates this because different heads can have different dominant positions.

Stage 3: Through the score computation and √dₖ

The scores are $S = \frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}$. Given $dS$ from the softmax backward:

$$dQ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_k}} \cdot dS \cdot K \qquad dK = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_k}} \cdot dS^T \cdot Q$$

Why √dₖ matters for gradients — the quantitative story

Without the $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling, the scores $S = QK^T$ have variance proportional to $d_k$. For $d_k = 64$, the standard deviation of scores is $\sqrt{64} = 8$. When these large-magnitude scores hit softmax, one element dominates and the output is nearly one-hot.

The softmax gradient at a near-one-hot distribution: $\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial S_j} = A_i(\delta_{ij} - A_j)$. If $A_i \approx 1$ for the dominant position, then $A_i(1 - A_i) \approx 0$ — the gradient for even the dominant element nearly vanishes. For all non-dominant positions, $A_j \approx 0$ gives gradient $\approx 0$ as well.

With scaling, the score variance is brought back to 1, keeping softmax in its non-saturated regime where gradients are healthy. This is exactly the same phenomenon as the sigmoid vanishing gradient problem (max derivative 0.25 at $z=0$, approaching 0 at large $|z|$), but applied to the attention distribution.

Left: with √dₖ scaling, attention is distributed and gradients flow to all positions. Right: without scaling, softmax saturates into a near one-hot distribution — gradients vanish everywhere.

04 — Enforcing CausalityGradient Through the Causal Mask

In autoregressive models, the causal mask sets scores at future positions to $-\infty$ before softmax. After softmax, those positions become exactly 0. The gradient consequence is direct: zero attention weight means zero gradient.

When $A_{ij} = 0$ (position $i$ doesn't attend to future position $j$), the softmax Jacobian gives $\frac{\partial A_{ij}}{\partial S_{ik}} = 0$ for all $k$. No gradient flows from position $i$'s output back through position $j$'s key or value. This means:

$$\text{Token } j \text{ receives zero gradient from any token } i < j$$

Causality is enforced in both directions — the forward pass can't see the future, and the backward pass can't send learning signals to the future either. A token's parameters are only updated based on how well they predicted the tokens that came after it, not based on tokens that came before it in the sequence.

Intuition
Think of the causal mask as a one-way mirror for gradients. Token 5 can attend to tokens 1-4 (and receives gradient based on how useful that attention was). But tokens 1-4 cannot attend to token 5, and token 5's key/value gradients come only from tokens 6, 7, 8, ... that attend to it. Each token learns to be a useful key/value for future tokens, and a good query for past tokens.

05 — Random SeveringGradient Through Attention Dropout

Transformers apply dropout in two distinct places, each with different gradient implications:

Attention dropout is applied to the attention weights $A$ (after softmax, before multiplying by $V$). During training, random attention connections are severed — some $A_{ij}$ are set to zero. The gradient for those connections is zero: no learning signal flows between the positions that were disconnected. The surviving connections are scaled by $\frac{1}{1-p}$ (inverted dropout), and their gradients are scaled identically.

Sublayer dropout is applied to the output of each sublayer (attention output, FFN output) before the residual addition. This drops random features in the representation, forcing the model to distribute information across multiple dimensions rather than relying on a few.

Why attention dropout prevents collapse
If attention dropout randomly zeros out the dominant attention weight during some training steps, the gradient for other positions is no longer zero in those steps. This breaks the rich-get-richer dynamic of attention collapse — the dominant position can't always collect gradient, giving other positions a chance to learn. This is distinct from the co-adaptation argument for standard dropout: attention dropout specifically prevents over-reliance on a single attention pattern.

06 — Architecture MattersPre-Norm vs Post-Norm Gradient Analysis

The placement of LayerNorm relative to the residual connection has a significant impact on gradient flow.

Post-norm (original transformer)

$$y = \text{LayerNorm}(x + f(x))$$

The gradient must pass through LayerNorm on the residual path:

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial \text{LN}}{\partial (x + f(x))} \cdot \left(I + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)$$

LayerNorm's Jacobian is not the identity — it re-centers and re-scales the gradient. While this prevents explosion, it also dampens the clean gradient highway that residuals provide. Over many layers, this dampening accumulates.

Pre-norm (GPT-2+, most modern models)

$$y = x + f(\text{LayerNorm}(x))$$

The gradient through the residual path:

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = I + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \text{LN}} \cdot \frac{\partial \text{LN}}{\partial x}$$

The identity $I$ is completely clean — no LayerNorm on the skip path. LayerNorm only appears inside the sub-layer branch. The gradient highway is unobstructed.

Post-norm places LayerNorm on the residual path, dampening the gradient highway. Pre-norm keeps the skip connection clean. The difference compounds over many layers.

07 — Sparse UpdatesGradient Through the Embedding Layer

The embedding layer is a lookup table: given token ID $t$, return row $t$ of the embedding matrix $E \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$. This is not a matrix multiplication — it's row selection.

How the gradient works

The gradient $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E}$ is a sparse matrix. Only the rows corresponding to tokens that appeared in the current batch receive non-zero gradients. If token "the" appears at positions 3, 7, and 15 in the batch, its gradient is the sum of upstream gradients from all three positions:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E[\text{"the"}]} = \sum_{i \in \{3, 7, 15\}} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_i}$$

All other rows of $E$ receive zero gradient — they're not updated this step.

Consequence: frequency-dependent learning

Common tokens ("the", "is", "of") get updated on nearly every batch. Rare tokens ("quintessential", "ephemeral") might go hundreds of batches without an update. This creates a severe imbalance in effective learning rates — common tokens get optimized heavily while rare tokens barely move.

Intuition
This is exactly why Adam is essential for transformer training. Adam's per-parameter adaptive learning rate compensates for different update frequencies. A rare token that gets updated infrequently accumulates a small second moment $v$, so when it finally does get a gradient, $\frac{m}{\sqrt{v} + \epsilon}$ produces a larger effective step. SGD has no such mechanism — rare tokens would be permanently under-trained.

08 — Position SignalsGradient Through Positional Encoding

The gradient behavior depends on which positional encoding scheme is used:

Sinusoidal (fixed)

The positional encoding $PE$ is a constant matrix added to the input: $x = E[\text{token}] + PE[\text{pos}]$. Since $PE$ is not a parameter, there's no gradient to compute for it. The gradient passes straight through the addition unchanged: $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E[\text{token}]} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x}$.

Learned (BERT, GPT-2)

The positional embedding is a learnable parameter matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$. Its gradient works exactly like the token embedding — row selection with sparse updates. Position 0's embedding gets updated every time a sequence of length $\geq 1$ is processed. Position 511 only gets updated when sequences are exactly 512 tokens long. This creates the same frequency imbalance as token embeddings.

RoPE (LLaMA, GPT-NeoX)

RoPE applies a position-dependent rotation to Q and K. The rotation matrix $R_m$ at position $m$ is orthogonal, meaning $R_m^{-1} = R_m^T$. The gradient through a rotation is:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q} = R_m^T \cdot \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (R_m q)}$$

Since rotation preserves vector norms ($\|R_m q\| = \|q\|$), the gradient magnitude is also preserved: $\left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q}\right\| = \left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (R_m q)}\right\|$. No scaling up or down. This is a nicer property than sinusoidal addition, where the gradient can be distorted by the interaction between the added positional signal and the token representation.

Key result
RoPE is gradient-neutral: it preserves both the forward magnitude (norm-preserving rotation) and the backward magnitude (orthogonal inverse). Sinusoidal PE adds a constant that doesn't interact with the gradient. Learned PE has the sparse-update frequency problem. From a gradient flow perspective, RoPE is the cleanest.

09 — One Matrix, Two GradientsGradient Through Weight Tying

In most modern transformers, the token embedding matrix $E$ and the output projection matrix are the same parameter (with the output projection using $E^T$). The forward pass uses $E$ in two places:

At the bottom: $\text{embed}(t) = E[t]$ (row lookup).

At the top: $\text{logits} = h \cdot E^T$ (matrix multiply to produce vocabulary-sized output).

During backprop, the gradient for $E$ comes from both paths. By the multi-path chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E}\bigg|_{\text{from embedding}}}_{\text{sparse: only active tokens}} + \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial E}\bigg|_{\text{from output projection}}}_{\text{dense: full matrix update}}$$

The embedding gradient is sparse (only rows for tokens in the batch). The output projection gradient is dense (the full matrix is used in the matmul). These get summed. This means even tokens that didn't appear in the batch receive gradient through the output projection path — they're being updated based on how their logit influenced the loss.

Intuition
Weight tying forces the embedding to be simultaneously good at two tasks: representing tokens (embedding) and predicting tokens (output projection). The gradient from the output projection says "this is what the final hidden states need to distinguish between tokens." The gradient from the embedding says "this is what the input representations need to look like." The shared matrix has to satisfy both constraints — which acts as a form of regularization.

10 — Softening the TargetGradient at the Output: Label Smoothing

Standard cross-entropy uses hard one-hot targets: the correct token gets probability 1, all others get 0. The gradient at the output logits is $\hat{y} - y$, which drives the correct logit toward $+\infty$ and all others toward $-\infty$.

Label smoothing replaces the hard target with a soft one:

$$y_{\text{smooth}}(i) = \begin{cases} 1 - \epsilon & \text{if } i = \text{correct} \\ \frac{\epsilon}{V - 1} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $\epsilon$ is typically 0.1 and $V$ is the vocabulary size. The gradient is still $\hat{y} - y_{\text{smooth}}$, but now the target for wrong classes is $\frac{\epsilon}{V-1}$ instead of 0. This means the gradient doesn't try to push wrong-class logits all the way to $-\infty$ — just "low enough."

Why this helps training

Without smoothing, the model becomes overconfident — it outputs near-1 probabilities for the correct token. This pushes the logits into the saturated regime of softmax, where gradients are small. With smoothing, the model is trained to output at most $(1 - \epsilon)$ probability, keeping logits in a well-conditioned regime where gradients remain healthy.

11 — Sparse RoutingGradient Through MoE Routing

In a Mixture of Experts layer, each token is routed to the top-$k$ experts selected by a gating network. This introduces two gradient challenges that don't exist in standard transformers.

Challenge 1: Non-differentiable top-k

The router computes scores $g(x) = \text{softmax}(W_g \cdot x)$ and selects the top-$k$ experts. The selection step (argmax / top-k) is non-differentiable — you can't backprop through "pick the two largest elements." The gradient only flows through the selected experts' scores and outputs:

$$\text{output} = \sum_{i \in \text{top-}k} g_i(x) \cdot E_i(x)$$

The gradient flows through $g_i$ (the router probability for selected experts) and through $E_i(x)$ (the expert outputs). Experts that were not selected receive zero gradient — their parameters are not updated, and the router's gradient for them is zero because they weren't in the computation graph.

Challenge 2: Dead experts

If the router learns to never select expert $j$, expert $j$ never gets gradient, so it never improves, so the router never selects it — a vicious cycle. This is the exact same problem as dead ReLU neurons: once a neuron's output is always zero, its gradient is always zero, and it can never recover.

The load-balancing loss

The auxiliary loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{balance}} = \alpha \cdot N \sum_{i=1}^{N} f_i \cdot P_i$ penalizes uneven expert utilization. The key gradient insight: the loss differentiates through $P_i$ (the average router probability, which depends on the softmax output), but not through $f_i$ (the fraction of tokens routed, which depends on the non-differentiable top-k).

This means the load-balancing gradient can only adjust the router's preferences (softmax probabilities), not the routing decisions (top-k selection) directly. The gradient signal says "increase the probability of underused experts" — and over many steps, this eventually shifts the top-k decisions because a previously-low probability expert gets high enough probability to enter the top-k.

Gradient only flows through selected experts (solid arrows). Unselected experts (dashed) get zero gradient. The load-balancing loss separately pushes the router to distribute tokens more evenly.

12 — The Big PictureTraining Dynamics: Warmup, Accumulation, and Why Gradients Grow

The counterintuitive problem: gradients grow, not vanish

Classical deep networks suffer from vanishing gradients. Transformers with residual connections have the opposite problem: each residual layer adds $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ to the gradient. In a 96-layer network, the gradient at the input is a sum of contributions from all layers — its magnitude can grow rather than shrink.

This is why learning rate warmup is essential for transformers. During early training, the model weights are random and the gradients are large and noisy. Starting with a small learning rate and gradually increasing it (typically over 1-5% of training) allows the model to settle into a region of the loss landscape where gradient norms are predictable. Without warmup, the first few steps take enormous steps in random directions, destabilizing training permanently.

Connection to your optimizer blog
Adam's bias correction also plays a role here. In early training, the second moment $v$ is initialized to zero and hasn't yet estimated the gradient magnitude. Without bias correction, the effective learning rate is too large in the first few steps. Warmup sidesteps this by keeping the actual learning rate small while $v$ accumulates meaningful statistics. This is the warmup-vs-bias-correction insight you studied earlier.

Gradient accumulation

Transformers need large effective batch sizes (thousands of sequences) for stable training, but GPU memory limits the batch that fits in one forward/backward pass. Gradient accumulation runs $K$ mini-batches, sums their gradients, then does one optimizer step — mathematically identical to a batch $K$ times larger.

The gradient subtlety: LayerNorm and dropout behave per micro-batch, not per accumulated batch. LayerNorm computes statistics over each micro-batch independently — the normalized activations and their gradients are slightly different than they'd be with a true large batch. This is fine for LayerNorm (since it normalizes per-token, batch size doesn't matter). But it would be a problem for BatchNorm (statistics depend on batch size) — yet another reason transformers use LayerNorm.

14 — Interview PrepInterview Questions

Question 1
Why can transformers be much deeper than RNNs?
Residual connections provide a gradient highway: the gradient at each layer is $I + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, meaning it's always at least 1 in magnitude. RNNs multiply gradients through time steps with no skip connections — each step the gradient is multiplied by the recurrent weight matrix, which causes exponential decay (vanishing) or growth (exploding). A 96-layer transformer's gradient includes a direct path from the loss to the input that bypasses all sub-layers entirely.
Question 2
Pre-norm vs post-norm — which is better for deep networks and why?
Pre-norm. In post-norm, LayerNorm sits on the residual path: $\text{LN}(x + f(x))$. The gradient must pass through LayerNorm's Jacobian on the skip connection, which re-centers and re-scales — dampening the clean gradient highway. In pre-norm, the residual path is pure addition: $x + f(\text{LN}(x))$. The identity gradient flows unobstructed. Over 96 layers, post-norm's dampening compounds. This is why GPT-2 switched from post-norm to pre-norm and all modern LLMs use pre-norm.
Question 3
What is attention collapse and how does it affect gradients?
Attention collapse is when the attention distribution becomes nearly one-hot — one position gets ~100% of the attention weight. The softmax Jacobian at a one-hot distribution is near-zero for all elements: $A_i(1-A_i) \approx 0$ for the dominant position and $A_j \approx 0$ for all others. This means gradients vanish for all positions — neither the dominant nor the non-dominant positions receive useful gradient signal. Multi-head attention mitigates this (different heads can have different dominant positions), and attention dropout randomly disrupts the collapse during training.
Question 4
Why does the √dₖ scaling matter for gradient flow?
Without scaling, the dot product $q \cdot k$ has variance $d_k$. For $d_k = 64$, the scores have standard deviation 8, which pushes softmax into saturation — the output is nearly one-hot. The softmax gradient in the saturated regime is near zero (same mechanism as sigmoid vanishing gradients). Scaling by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the score variance back to 1, keeping softmax in its non-saturated regime where gradients are $O(1)$.
Question 5
How does weight tying affect the gradient for the embedding matrix?
With weight tying, the embedding matrix $E$ receives gradient from two paths: (1) the embedding lookup at the bottom (sparse — only rows for tokens in the batch are updated) and (2) the output projection at the top (dense — the full matrix participates in the matmul). These gradients are summed by the multi-path chain rule. This means even tokens not in the batch receive gradient through the output path. The shared matrix must simultaneously be good at representing tokens and predicting them — this dual objective acts as regularization.
Question 6
Why is Adam important for transformer training? What happens with SGD?
The embedding layer creates severe gradient sparsity: common tokens get updated every batch, rare tokens go hundreds of batches without updates. Adam's per-parameter adaptive learning rate compensates — rare tokens accumulate a small second moment $v$, so their effective step size $m/(\sqrt{v}+\epsilon)$ is larger when they finally do get a gradient. SGD has no such mechanism: all parameters share the same learning rate, so rare tokens are permanently under-trained. Additionally, Adam's momentum helps smooth the noisy gradients that arise from the stochastic nature of which tokens appear in each batch.
Question 7
How do gradients flow through the causal mask?
The causal mask sets future positions' attention scores to $-\infty$ before softmax, giving them weight 0. The gradient for zero-weight positions is exactly zero — no learning signal flows from position $i$ to any future position $j > i$. This enforces causality in both directions: the forward pass can't see the future, and the backward pass can't send gradient to the future. Each token's key/value parameters are only updated based on how useful they were to tokens that came after it.
Question 8
Why is learning rate warmup critical for transformers?
Transformers with residual connections can have growing gradients (not vanishing), because each residual adds to the gradient magnitude. In early training with random weights, these gradients are large and noisy. Without warmup, the first optimizer steps take enormous strides in random directions, destabilizing training permanently. Warmup starts with a small learning rate, gradually increasing it over 1-5% of training, allowing Adam's second moment $v$ to estimate gradient variance and the model to settle into a well-conditioned region of the loss landscape.
Question 9
What's the gradient problem with MoE routing and how does the load-balancing loss help?
The top-k expert selection is non-differentiable — you can't backprop through argmax. Unselected experts get zero gradient, creating a dead-expert problem (same as dead ReLU neurons). The load-balancing loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{balance}} = \alpha N \sum f_i P_i$ differentiates through $P_i$ (router softmax probabilities) but not $f_i$ (routing fractions from top-k). It pushes the router to increase probabilities for underused experts. Over many steps, this gradually shifts which experts enter the top-k, reviving dead experts.
Question 10
How does RoPE affect gradient flow compared to sinusoidal or learned positional encoding?
RoPE applies an orthogonal rotation to Q and K. Since rotation matrices are orthogonal ($R^{-1} = R^T$), the gradient magnitude is perfectly preserved: $\|dq\| = \|d(Rq)\|$. No scaling up or down. Sinusoidal PE adds a constant — the gradient passes through unchanged but the addition can create interference between positional and semantic gradients in early layers. Learned PE has sparse-update frequency problems (position 0 is updated every step, position 511 rarely). From a gradient perspective, RoPE is the cleanest — gradient-neutral by construction.
Question 11
Why does label smoothing help transformer training?
Without label smoothing, the cross-entropy gradient drives the correct-class logit toward $+\infty$ and all others toward $-\infty$. This pushes softmax into saturation, where its gradient is near zero — the model stops learning even though it's overconfident. Label smoothing uses soft targets (e.g., 0.9 for the correct class, 0.1/(V-1) for others), which keeps the logits in a moderate range where softmax gradients remain healthy. The model converges to a well-calibrated distribution rather than an overconfident one.
Question 12
Why do transformers use LayerNorm instead of BatchNorm?
Three gradient-related reasons: (1) LayerNorm normalizes per-token across features, so each token's gradient is independent of other tokens in the batch — BatchNorm couples all samples' gradients, which is problematic with variable sequence lengths. (2) LayerNorm works identically with batch size 1, which matters for inference and for gradient accumulation (micro-batches have different sizes than the effective batch). (3) LayerNorm has no train/inference discrepancy — BatchNorm uses running statistics at inference, meaning the gradient paths during training don't match the computation at inference.
