The Attention Revolution:
From RNNs to Self-Attention

How and why Transformers replaced recurrent architectures — with the math, diagrams, and intuition interviewers expect

1. The Sequential Bottleneck: Why RNNs Weren't Enough

Before 2017, sequence modeling was dominated by recurrent neural networks and their gated variants — LSTMs and GRUs. These architectures had a fundamental design constraint that limited everything downstream: they processed sequences one token at a time, left to right.

1.1 Vanilla RNNs and the Vanishing Gradient Problem

A vanilla RNN maintains a hidden state \(h_t\) that gets updated at each timestep:

RNN Update Rule
$$h_t = \tanh(W_{hh} \cdot h_{t-1} + W_{xh} \cdot x_t + b)$$ $$y_t = W_{hy} \cdot h_t$$

The hidden state \(h_t\) is supposed to carry all relevant information from the past. During backpropagation through time (BPTT), gradients flow backwards through the chain of hidden states. The gradient of the loss with respect to an earlier hidden state involves a product of Jacobians:

Gradient Chain (BPTT)
$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h_t} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h_T} \cdot \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\partial h_{k+1}}{\partial h_k}$$

Each factor \(\frac{\partial h_{k+1}}{\partial h_k}\) involves multiplying by \(W_{hh}\) and the derivative of tanh. When the spectral radius of \(W_{hh}\) is less than 1, this product shrinks exponentially — gradients vanish. When it's greater than 1, they explode. Bengio et al. (1994) formally characterized this problem, showing that for vanilla RNNs, learning dependencies beyond roughly 10–20 timesteps was practically infeasible.

This isn't just a theoretical concern. For machine translation, a sentence like "The cat, which was sitting on the mat that my grandmother bought from the antique store last summer, was purring" requires the model to connect "cat" to "was purring" across many intervening tokens. Vanilla RNNs simply cannot learn this.

Figure 1: Vanishing gradients in vanilla RNNs. The gradient signal shrinks exponentially as it flows backward through time, making it impossible to learn long-range dependencies.

1.2 LSTM: The Gating Solution (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997)

📄
Long Short-Term Memory
Hochreiter & Schmidhuber · Neural Computation, 1997

Long Short-Term Memory networks addressed the vanishing gradient problem through a key architectural insight: create a dedicated memory channel (the cell state) with carefully controlled access.

The LSTM unit has four components:

LSTM Equations
$$f_t = \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f) \quad \textit{(forget gate — what to discard)}$$ $$i_t = \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) \quad \textit{(input gate — what to store)}$$ $$\tilde{c}_t = \tanh(W_c \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_c) \quad \textit{(candidate value)}$$ $$\boxed{c_t = f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t} \quad \textit{(cell state update — the key equation)}$$ $$o_t = \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o) \quad \textit{(output gate — what to expose)}$$ $$h_t = o_t \odot \tanh(c_t) \quad \textit{(hidden state output)}$$
Figure 2: LSTM cell architecture. The cell state (green highway) flows with only element-wise operations — the forget gate controls erasure (×), the input gate controls writing (+), and the output gate controls reading (× tanh).
💡 Why this fixes vanishing gradients

The cell state update \(c_t = f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t\) is the key. When the forget gate \(f_t \approx 1\) and the input gate \(i_t \approx 0\), the cell state passes through nearly unchanged: \(c_t \approx c_{t-1}\). During backpropagation, the gradient through the cell state involves multiplying by \(f_t\) (a value between 0 and 1 controlled by a learned gate), not by a fixed weight matrix:

$$\frac{\partial c_t}{\partial c_{t-1}} = f_t$$

This creates a gradient highway — the network can learn to keep the forget gate open, allowing gradients to flow across hundreds of timesteps without vanishing. This is conceptually similar to residual connections in later architectures (He et al., 2016): \(\frac{\partial(x + f(x))}{\partial x} = 1 + f'(x)\), which Transformers would also adopt.

The forget gate was actually not in the original 1997 paper — it was added by Gers, Schmidhuber, and Cummins (2000) in "Learning to Forget: Continual Prediction with LSTM." The original LSTM had the cell state always accumulating, which could cause it to grow unboundedly. The forget gate gave the network the ability to reset its memory when appropriate.

What LSTM still couldn't solve:

Figure 3: The three fundamental limitations of recurrent architectures — all addressed by self-attention.

Despite the gradient highway, information still degrades over very long sequences in practice. The hidden state \(h_t\) is a fixed-size vector that must compress all relevant past information. For a 1000-token document, \(h_{500}\) is expected to carry information about tokens 1 through 500 — that's an enormous amount of compression into a single vector.

And in seq2seq architectures (Sutskever et al., 2014), the entire source sentence gets compressed into a single fixed-length vector — the final hidden state of the encoder. Long sentences lose critical information in this compression.

1.3 GRU: Simplifying the Gates (Cho et al., 2014)

📄
Learning Phrase Representations using RNN Encoder-Decoder
Cho, van Merriënboer, Gulcehre, Bahdanau, Bougares, Schwenk & Bengio · EMNLP 2014

The Gated Recurrent Unit simplified the LSTM by merging the forget and input gates into a single update gate \(z_t\), and combining the cell state and hidden state into one vector:

GRU Equations
$$z_t = \sigma(W_z \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t]) \quad \textit{(update gate)}$$ $$r_t = \sigma(W_r \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t]) \quad \textit{(reset gate)}$$ $$\tilde{h}_t = \tanh(W \cdot [r_t \odot h_{t-1}, x_t]) \quad \textit{(candidate)}$$ $$h_t = (1 - z_t) \odot h_{t-1} + z_t \odot \tilde{h}_t \quad \textit{(output)}$$

The update gate \(z_t\) controls the tradeoff: when \(z_t \approx 0\), the hidden state copies forward (like LSTM's forget gate being 1); when \(z_t \approx 1\), the new candidate replaces the old state. The reset gate \(r_t\) controls how much past information to use when computing the candidate.

GRU achieved comparable performance to LSTM on many benchmarks with fewer parameters and slightly faster computation. But it shared the same fundamental limitations: sequential processing and a fixed-size hidden state bottleneck.

1.4 CNNs for Sequences: Parallelism Without Global Context

Before Transformers, researchers also explored convolutional approaches for sequences. Gehring et al. (2017) proposed the ConvS2S architecture, which used stacked CNNs instead of RNNs. Convolutions can process all positions in parallel (solving the parallelization problem), but they have a limited receptive field — each convolutional layer can only "see" a local window of tokens.

To capture long-range dependencies, you need to stack many layers: a kernel of size \(k\) over \(L\) layers gives a receptive field of \(O(k \times L)\). This means distant tokens can only interact through a deep chain of intermediate computations, which is less direct than what attention would provide.

2. The Attention Mechanism: Breaking the Bottleneck

2.1 Bahdanau Attention — The Original Breakthrough (2014)

📄
Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate
Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio · ICLR 2015 (arXiv September 2014)

The seq2seq bottleneck — compressing an entire source sentence into a single fixed-length vector — was first directly addressed by Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio. This paper introduced what we now call the attention mechanism, arguably the single most important idea leading to Transformers.

The problem it solved: In standard encoder-decoder models, the decoder receives only the encoder's final hidden state. For long sentences, this single vector cannot preserve all the information needed for accurate translation. Bahdanau et al. observed that translation quality degraded significantly as sentence length increased beyond 20–30 tokens.

The solution — let the decoder look back at all encoder states: Instead of passing only the last hidden state, Bahdanau attention passes all encoder hidden states to the decoder. At each decoding step \(t\), the decoder computes a context vector \(c_t\) as a weighted sum of all encoder hidden states:

Bahdanau Attention (Additive)
$$e_{tj} = \mathbf{v}_a^\top \tanh(W_a \cdot s_{t-1} + U_a \cdot h_j) \quad \textit{(alignment score)}$$ $$\alpha_{tj} = \frac{\exp(e_{tj})}{\sum_k \exp(e_{tk})} \quad \textit{(softmax → attention weights)}$$ $$c_t = \sum_j \alpha_{tj} \cdot h_j \quad \textit{(context vector — weighted sum)}$$

Here, \(s_{t-1}\) is the previous decoder hidden state, \(h_j\) is the \(j\)-th encoder hidden state, and the alignment model is a learned feedforward network. This is called additive attention because the score function adds the transformed decoder and encoder states before applying tanh.

Figure 4: Bahdanau attention when decoding "cat". The model attends strongly to "chat" (α = 0.85), pulling the relevant French word's representation into the context vector. The encoder is bidirectional, so each h_j captures both left and right context.

Key insight: The encoder uses a bidirectional RNN, so each annotation \(h_j = [\overrightarrow{h_j} ; \overleftarrow{h_j}]\) captures context from both before and after position \(j\). The attention weights \(\alpha_{tj}\) form a soft alignment — they tell the decoder which source words to focus on when generating each target word.

Why this was revolutionary:

  1. No more fixed-length bottleneck: Information can be spread across the entire sequence of annotations and selectively retrieved.
  2. Soft alignment is differentiable: The entire system, including the alignment model, is trained end-to-end with backpropagation. No explicit alignment labels needed.
  3. Interpretability: The attention weights provide a natural visualization of which source words the model is "looking at" for each target word.

Bahdanau et al. demonstrated that their model handled long sentences dramatically better than the fixed-vector encoder-decoder, with BLEU scores remaining stable even for sentences of 50+ tokens where the baseline collapsed.

2.2 Luong Attention — Simplifying and Generalizing (2015)

📄
Effective Approaches to Attention-based Neural Machine Translation
Luong, Pham & Manning · EMNLP 2015

Luong, Pham, and Manning built on Bahdanau's work, introducing two variants:

Global attention (attends to all source positions, like Bahdanau) but with simpler scoring functions:

Luong Attention Scores (3 variants)
$$\text{dot:} \quad \text{score}(s_t, h_j) = s_t^\top h_j$$ $$\text{general:} \quad \text{score}(s_t, h_j) = s_t^\top W_a h_j$$ $$\text{concat:} \quad \text{score}(s_t, h_j) = v_a^\top \tanh(W_a [s_t ; h_j])$$

Local attention attends to a window around a predicted alignment point, reducing computational cost for long sequences. It takes inspiration from the hard and soft attention models of Xu et al. (2015) for image captioning.

The key distinction from Bahdanau: Luong attention uses the current decoder hidden state \(s_t\) to compute alignment (not the previous state \(s_{t-1}\)), and applies the multiplicative (dot-product) scoring function. This multiplicative attention is computationally cheaper — a simple dot product vs. Bahdanau's feedforward network — and became the basis for the Transformer's scaled dot-product attention.

PropertyBahdanau (Additive)Luong (Multiplicative)
Score function\(v^\top \tanh(Ws_{t-1} + Uh_j)\)\(s_t^\top h_j\) or \(s_t^\top W h_j\)
ParametersTwo weight matrices + vectorOne matrix (or none for dot)
Computational costHigher (FF network + tanh)Lower (matrix multiply / dot)
Decoder statePrevious (\(s_{t-1}\))Current (\(s_t\))
EncoderBidirectional RNNUnidirectional stacked LSTM
→ TransformerScaled dot-product: \(Q K^\top / \sqrt{d_k}\)

Both are cross-attention mechanisms — the query comes from the decoder and the keys/values come from the encoder. This is the same structure the Transformer's decoder cross-attention layer uses, except the Transformer replaces the RNN hidden states with self-attention outputs.

2.3 The Remaining Problem: Attention + RNN Was Still Sequential

Bahdanau and Luong attention solved the information bottleneck but not the sequential computation problem. The decoder still generates one token at a time, and each step still depends on the previous hidden state. The encoder (especially bidirectional) must process the full sequence before the decoder can start. Training cannot be fully parallelized.

⚠️ The remaining gap

Attention was doing the heavy lifting for performance — but the RNN was still the computational bottleneck, preventing scaling and parallelization. This sets the stage: what if we use attention not just between encoder and decoder, but within them, replacing recurrence entirely?

3. Self-Attention: The Transformer's Core Innovation

3.1 From Cross-Attention to Self-Attention

📄
Attention Is All You Need
Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit, Jones, Gomez, Kaiser & Polosukhin · NeurIPS 2017 (arXiv June 2017)

In Bahdanau/Luong attention, the query comes from one sequence (decoder) and the keys/values come from another (encoder). Self-attention applies this same idea within a single sequence: every token attends to every other token in the same sequence. This was the core proposal of Vaswani et al. (2017).

The Transformer paper made a bold claim: you can dispense with recurrence and convolutions entirely, building a sequence model using only attention and feedforward layers. The key arguments were:

1. Parallelism: Self-attention computes all pairwise relationships simultaneously. For a sequence of length \(n\), instead of \(O(n)\) sequential steps (RNN) or \(O(n/k)\) steps with kernel size \(k\) (CNN), self-attention needs \(O(1)\) sequential steps — all positions are processed in parallel. Training becomes dramatically faster.

2. Path length for long-range dependencies: In an RNN, information from position 1 must traverse \(O(n)\) steps to reach position \(n\). In a CNN, it traverses \(O(\log_k n)\) layers. In self-attention, every pair of positions is connected in a single layer — the maximum path length is \(O(1)\). This makes learning long-range dependencies fundamentally easier.

3. Computational complexity tradeoffs: Self-attention has \(O(n^2 \cdot d)\) complexity per layer, vs. \(O(n \cdot d^2)\) for recurrent layers and \(O(k \cdot n \cdot d^2)\) for convolutional layers. When \(n < d\) (typical for NLP), self-attention is actually faster. The quadratic cost in \(n\) only becomes problematic for very long sequences.

Figure 5: RNN (sequential, O(n) hops between distant tokens) vs Self-Attention (parallel, every token connects to every other directly in one step).

The original Transformer demonstrated these advantages on machine translation: 28.4 BLEU on English-to-German (WMT 2014), surpassing all previous results including ensembles, after training for only 3.5 days on 8 GPUs. Previous state-of-the-art models took weeks to train.

⚡ Why O(n²) beats O(n) in practice

The \(O(n^2)\) is in total operations, but they're all independent — massively parallelizable on GPUs. The RNN's \(O(n)\) is in sequential steps where each depends on the previous. For typical NLP lengths (\(n < 512\)), parallel \(O(n^2)\) on a GPU is far faster than sequential \(O(n)\). Plus, when \(d > n\), the RNN's total FLOPs \(O(n \cdot d^2)\) actually exceed self-attention's \(O(n^2 \cdot d)\).

3.2 Why Self-Attention Is Not Just "Better RNNs"

Self-attention is a fundamentally different computational primitive than recurrence:

RNNs: Each position's representation is a function of the previous position's representation and the current input. Information flows left-to-right (or both directions in BiLSTMs). The hidden state acts as a sequential memory that gets updated incrementally.

Self-attention: Each position's representation is a function of all positions' representations, computed simultaneously. There's no notion of sequential order (which is why positional encoding is needed — covered in Blog 4). Each position directly queries every other position and decides what information to incorporate.

An RNN at position \(t\) has seen tokens 1 through \(t\) in order. Self-attention at position \(t\) has seen all tokens simultaneously and weighted them by relevance. It's the difference between reading a book page by page vs. seeing all pages at once and choosing which to focus on.

6. Interview Questions You Must Be Ready For

Q1 — Conceptual
Why can't vanilla RNNs learn long-range dependencies?
BPTT involves multiplying gradients across timesteps: \(\prod \frac{\partial h_{k+1}}{\partial h_k}\). Each factor involves \(W_{hh}\) and the derivative of tanh. When the spectral radius of \(W_{hh} < 1\), the product shrinks exponentially (vanishing gradients), making it impossible to learn connections between distant tokens. Bengio et al. (1994) showed learning beyond ~10–20 steps is infeasible.
Q2 — Conceptual
How does the LSTM cell state solve vanishing gradients?
The cell state update \(c_t = f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t\) is additive with a learned gate. When \(f_t \approx 1\), gradients flow through the cell state with minimal attenuation: \(\partial c_t / \partial c_{t-1} = f_t\), which the network learns to keep close to 1. This creates a "gradient highway" analogous to residual connections. Unlike vanilla RNNs where gradients pass through tanh and \(W_{hh}\) at every step, the LSTM cell state provides a direct, additive path.
Q3 — Conceptual
What are the three fundamental limitations of RNNs that Transformers address?
(1) Sequential computation — O(n) sequential steps, can't parallelize training. (2) Long-range dependency — information degrades over long sequences despite LSTM/GRU gating. (3) Fixed-size bottleneck — the hidden state must compress all past information into one vector of fixed dimension.
Q4 — Comparison
What's the difference between Bahdanau (additive) and Luong (multiplicative) attention?
Bahdanau: score = \(v^\top \tanh(Ws_{t-1} + Uh_j)\) — feedforward network, uses previous decoder state, bidirectional encoder. Luong: score = \(s_t^\top h_j\) or \(s_t^\top Wh_j\) — dot product, uses current decoder state. Multiplicative is cheaper and became the basis for Transformer attention (with \(1/\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling). Both solve the fixed-length bottleneck by allowing the decoder to attend to all encoder states.
Q5 — Complexity
If self-attention is O(n²) and RNNs are O(n), why is self-attention faster?
\(O(n^2)\) = total ops, all parallelizable on GPUs. \(O(n)\) for RNNs = sequential steps, each waiting for the previous. For NLP (\(n < 512\)), parallel \(O(n^2)\) far outpaces sequential \(O(n)\). Also, when \(d > n\), RNN's total FLOPs \(O(n \cdot d^2)\) exceed self-attention's \(O(n^2 \cdot d)\).
Q6 — Tradeoffs
When might you still choose an RNN over a Transformer?
(1) Very long sequences where \(O(n^2)\) memory is prohibitive and efficient attention variants don't apply. (2) Streaming/online inference — token-by-token without KV-cache overhead. (3) Extremely resource-constrained devices. (4) Tasks requiring strict sequential inductive bias (though Transformers with positional encoding handle this well in practice).
Q7 — Architecture
How does Bahdanau attention relate to the Transformer's cross-attention?
Conceptually identical: queries from decoder, keys/values from encoder, soft alignment via weighted sum. The differences are implementation: Bahdanau uses a learned FF network for scoring on RNN hidden states; the Transformer uses scaled dot-product attention on learned projections, with multi-head attention for diverse patterns. The Transformer's cross-attention is Bahdanau attention re-implemented without the RNN.
Q8 — Motivation
What motivated the jump from "attention as an add-on" to "attention as the entire architecture"?
Three converging insights: (1) Attention was doing the heavy lifting — ablation studies showed performance gains came primarily from attention, not the RNN. (2) The RNN was the computational bottleneck — its sequential nature limited training speed and model scale. (3) Self-attention could replace the RNN's role of building contextual representations, computing all pairwise relationships in one step.
