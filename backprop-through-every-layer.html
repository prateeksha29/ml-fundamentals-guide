When you call loss.backward() in PyTorch, dozens of gradient computations fire in sequence. Each layer type — loss functions, activations, normalization, regularization — has its own backward pass with its own derivation.

This article derives every one of them from scratch. No code, no hand-waving. Just the math and the intuition behind it, so that when you see a gradient formula, you know exactly where it came from and why it looks the way it does.

We'll start with the simplest case and build up. By the end, you'll have derived the backward pass for the five most important building blocks in deep learning.

01 — PrerequisiteThe Chain Rule Refresher

Every derivation in this article relies on one idea: the chain rule. If you have a composition of functions $$L = f(g(h(x)))$$, the derivative of $$L$$ with respect to $$x$$ is:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot \frac{\partial f}{\partial g} \cdot \frac{\partial g}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}$$

In a neural network, information flows forward through layers, and gradients flow backward through the same layers in reverse order. At each layer, we receive an upstream gradient (what came from the layers above us) and we need to compute two things:

Parameter gradients — how much should this layer's weights change? This is what the optimizer uses.

Downstream gradient — what gradient should we pass to the layer below us? This keeps the chain going.

Intuition
Think of backprop like a blame game. The loss says "something went wrong." Each layer passes the blame backward, saying "here's how much of this was my fault" (parameter gradients) and "here's how much was your fault" (downstream gradient to the previous layer).

With that, let's derive our first backward pass.

02 — Binary ClassificationBackprop Through BCE + Sigmoid

In binary classification, the last two operations in the forward pass are the sigmoid activation and the binary cross-entropy loss. We need the gradient of the loss with respect to the pre-activation logits — the values before sigmoid is applied.

Setting up the forward pass

The sigmoid function squashes any real number into $$(0, 1)$$:

$$\hat{y} = \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$$

Then binary cross-entropy measures how far these probabilities are from the true labels:

$$L = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[y_i \log \hat{y}_i + (1 - y_i)\log(1 - \hat{y}_i)\right]$$

We want $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z}$$. By the chain rule, this decomposes into two pieces:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial z}$$

Piece 1: The sigmoid derivative

The sigmoid has one of the cleanest derivatives in all of calculus. Starting from $$\sigma(z) = (1 + e^{-z})^{-1}$$:

Deriving the sigmoid derivative
1
Apply the power rule and chain rule: $$\frac{d\sigma}{dz} = -1 \cdot (1 + e^{-z})^{-2} \cdot (-e^{-z})= \frac{e^{-z}}{(1 + e^{-z})^2}$$
2
Split the fraction into two factors: $$= \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \cdot \frac{e^{-z}}{1 + e^{-z}}$$
3
Recognize the first factor as $$\sigma(z)$$ and the second as $$1 - \sigma(z)$$: $$\frac{d\sigma}{dz} = \sigma(z)\bigl(1 - \sigma(z)\bigr)$$

This means the derivative is entirely expressible in terms of the output of sigmoid, which is convenient — we've already computed it during the forward pass.

Watch out
The maximum value of $$\sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$$ is $$0.25$$, occurring at $$z = 0$$. This means every sigmoid layer at best scales the gradient down by a factor of 4. Stack ten sigmoid layers and the gradient shrinks by $$0.25^{10} \approx 10^{-6}$$. This is the vanishing gradient problem.
The sigmoid function (solid) and its derivative (dashed). The derivative peaks at 0.25 — gradients can never grow through a sigmoid layer.

Piece 2: The BCE derivative

Differentiating the BCE loss with respect to $$\hat{y}$$ (treating a single sample for clarity):

Deriving the BCE gradient
1
Start with: $$L = -\left[y\log\hat{y} + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})\right]$$
2
Differentiate each term: $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = -\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}$$
3
Combine over a common denominator: $$= \frac{-y(1-\hat{y}) + (1-y)\hat{y}}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})} = \frac{\hat{y} - y}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})}$$

The magic: putting them together

Now we chain the two pieces together. This is where something beautiful happens:

The cancellation
1
Chain rule: $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial z}$$
2
Substitute both: $$= \frac{\hat{y} - y}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})} \cdot \hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$$
3
The $$\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$$ cancels completely:
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = \hat{y} - y$$
Intuition
The gradient is simply the prediction error. If the network predicts 0.9 and the true label is 1, the gradient is just $$-0.1$$. No complex terms, no scaling issues. This is not a coincidence — BCE was specifically designed to pair with sigmoid to produce this clean gradient. It's also numerically stable: the separate form divides by $$\hat{y}(1 - \hat{y})$$, which explodes when predictions are near 0 or 1. The combined form avoids this entirely.

03 — Multi-class ClassificationBackprop Through Softmax + Cross-Entropy

The multi-class version of what we just did. Instead of sigmoid squashing to one probability, softmax produces a probability distribution over $$C$$ classes:

$$\hat{y}_i = \text{softmax}(z)_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{C} e^{z_j}}$$

And the multi-class cross-entropy loss (with one-hot labels $$y$$) is:

$$L = -\sum_{i=1}^{C} y_i \log \hat{y}_i$$

We want $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k}$$ — the gradient with respect to each logit.

The softmax Jacobian

Unlike sigmoid (which is element-wise), softmax couples all outputs — changing one logit affects every probability. So we need a full Jacobian matrix. For output $$\hat{y}_i$$ with respect to input $$z_k$$:

Softmax Jacobian derivation
1
Case $$i = k$$ (diagonal): Using the quotient rule on $$\hat{y}_i = e^{z_i} / S$$ where $$S = \sum e^{z_j}$$: $$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_i} = \frac{e^{z_i} \cdot S - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_i}}{S^2} = \hat{y}_i - \hat{y}_i^2 = \hat{y}_i(1 - \hat{y}_i)$$
2
Case $$i \neq k$$ (off-diagonal): The numerator $$e^{z_i}$$ doesn't depend on $$z_k$$, so only $$S$$ contributes: $$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k} = \frac{0 - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_k}}{S^2} = -\hat{y}_i \hat{y}_k$$
3
Compact form using the Kronecker delta $$\delta_{ik}$$: $$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k} = \hat{y}_i(\delta_{ik} - \hat{y}_k)$$

Chaining with cross-entropy

The cross-entropy gradient with respect to $$\hat{y}_i$$ is straightforward: $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}_i} = -\frac{y_i}{\hat{y}_i}$$. Now we chain:

Combined softmax + CE gradient
1
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \sum_{i=1}^{C} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k}$$ (sum because each $$\hat{y}_i$$ depends on $$z_k$$)
2
Substitute: $$= \sum_i \left(-\frac{y_i}{\hat{y}_i}\right) \cdot \hat{y}_i(\delta_{ik} - \hat{y}_k) = -\sum_i y_i(\delta_{ik} - \hat{y}_k)$$
3
Expand: $$= -y_k + \hat{y}_k\sum_i y_i$$. Since $$y$$ is one-hot, $$\sum y_i = 1$$:
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \hat{y}_k - y_k$$

The exact same form as sigmoid + BCE. Prediction minus truth. This stunning symmetry is one of the most elegant results in machine learning.

Intuition
In both the binary and multi-class cases, the gradient is simply "how wrong you were." If the true class is class 3 and you predicted $$[0.1, 0.2, 0.6, 0.1]$$, the gradient is $$[0.1, 0.2, -0.4, 0.1]$$. The predicted-class logit gets pushed down (because $$0.6 - 1 = -0.4$$), and all wrong-class logits get pushed up. The network learns to redistribute probability mass toward the correct answer.

04 — NormalizationBackprop Through Batch Normalization

Batch normalization is applied between the linear transformation and the activation function. Its forward pass has three stages: normalize, scale, shift.

$$\mu = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i \qquad \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \mu)^2$$
$$\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} \qquad y_i = \gamma\hat{x}_i + \beta$$

The backward pass is notoriously tricky because $$\mu$$ and $$\sigma^2$$ are themselves functions of all the inputs. Every input contributes to the mean and variance, which means differentiating through them couples every sample in the batch.

The easy gradients first

The learnable parameters $$\gamma$$ (scale) and $$\beta$$ (shift) have simple gradients, since $$y_i = \gamma\hat{x}_i + \beta$$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \gamma} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_i} \cdot \hat{x}_i \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \beta} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_i}$$

The hard gradient: $$\partial L / \partial x_i$$

This is where the challenge lies. We need to differentiate through three interacting paths: $$x_i$$ appears directly in the numerator of $$\hat{x}_i$$, but it also affects $$\mu$$ and $$\sigma^2$$, which are in the denominator. We need to account for all three paths.

The three paths through which xᵢ influences the output. Backprop must account for all three.

Rather than tracking each path separately (which is valid but tedious), we can use a shortcut. Let $$d\hat{x}_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_i} \cdot \gamma$$ be the gradient that has passed through $$\gamma$$. Then the full input gradient, after combining all three paths through the computational graph, simplifies to:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = \frac{1}{N} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}}\left(N \cdot d\hat{x}_i - \sum_{j} d\hat{x}_j - \hat{x}_i \sum_{j} d\hat{x}_j \cdot \hat{x}_j\right)$$

This formula packs three contributions into one expression. Let's unpack each term:

The first term, $$N \cdot d\hat{x}_i$$, is the direct path — the gradient flowing straight through the normalization as if $$\mu$$ and $$\sigma^2$$ were constants.

The second term, $$-\sum d\hat{x}_j$$, is the mean correction. Because $$x_i$$ contributes to $$\mu$$, which shifts every sample, we subtract the average gradient. This ensures the output gradients are zero-centered.

The third term, $$-\hat{x}_i \sum d\hat{x}_j \hat{x}_j$$, is the variance correction. Because $$x_i$$ contributes to $$\sigma^2$$, which scales every sample, we subtract a term proportional to how far $$x_i$$ was from the mean.

Intuition
BatchNorm's backward pass does something rather elegant: it re-normalizes the gradients too. The output gradients are guaranteed to have zero mean and a specific variance structure — just like the forward pass normalized the activations. This is part of why BatchNorm stabilizes training so effectively. Gradients that pass through it can't blow up or collapse.
Interview note
A common follow-up question: "What's different at inference time?" During training, you use per-batch $$\mu$$ and $$\sigma^2$$. During inference, you use a running average of these statistics collected during training. The parameters $$\gamma$$ and $$\beta$$ are always the same. This means the backward pass only exists during training — at inference time, BatchNorm is just a fixed linear transformation.

05 — Regularization IBackprop Through Dropout

Dropout is the simplest backward pass to derive, but its design involves a subtlety that trips people up: the inverted scaling.

Forward pass: inverted dropout

During training, each neuron is independently dropped with probability $$p$$. The surviving neurons are scaled up by $$\frac{1}{1-p}$$:

$$a_i^{\text{dropped}} = \frac{m_i \cdot a_i}{1-p} \qquad \text{where } m_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1-p)$$

During inference, dropout is turned off entirely — activations pass through unchanged.

Why the $$1/(1-p)$$ scaling?

Without it, there's an expectation mismatch between training and inference.

Inverted dropout scales surviving neurons during training so that expected values match inference without any modification at test time.

The backward pass

Since the forward pass is just element-wise multiplication and scaling, the backward pass is identical in form:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial a_i} = \frac{m_i}{1 - p} \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_i^{\text{dropped}}}$$

The same mask, the same scaling. If a neuron was dropped during the forward pass, its gradient is zero — no learning signal flows through dead neurons. If it survived, the gradient is scaled up by $$\frac{1}{1-p}$$, just like the activation was.

Key result
Dropout's backward pass is symmetric with its forward pass: multiply by the same mask, divide by the same $$(1-p)$$. The mask must be saved from the forward pass and reused — you cannot generate a new random mask during backprop.

06 — Regularization IIBackprop Through L2 Regularization

L2 regularization (weight decay) adds a penalty to the loss that discourages large weights. Unlike the other sections, this modifies the loss function itself rather than adding a new layer to backprop through.

The modified loss

$$L_{\text{reg}} = L_{\text{data}} + \frac{\lambda}{2N}\sum_l \|W^{[l]}\|_F^2$$

The $$\frac{1}{2}$$ is a convenience factor — it cancels when we differentiate the squared term. $$\lambda$$ controls regularization strength, and $$\|W\|_F^2 = \sum_{ij} w_{ij}^2$$ is the squared Frobenius norm.

The modified gradient

Since the regularization term only involves weights (not activations, not biases), it only adds to the weight gradients:

L2 gradient derivation
1
The regularization term for layer $$l$$ is $$\frac{\lambda}{2N}\sum_{ij}(w_{ij}^{[l]})^2$$
2
Differentiating w.r.t. $$W^{[l]}$$: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial W^{[l]}}\frac{\lambda}{2N}\|W^{[l]}\|_F^2 = \frac{\lambda}{N} W^{[l]}$$
3
Add this to the existing data gradient:
$$\frac{\partial L_{\text{reg}}}{\partial W^{[l]}} = \frac{\partial L_{\text{data}}}{\partial W^{[l]}} + \frac{\lambda}{N}W^{[l]}$$

The gradient now has two components: the data term (which tells the network what the data wants) and the regularization term (which pushes weights toward zero).

The weight decay perspective

When you expand what SGD does with this gradient, something illuminating happens:

$$W \leftarrow W - \alpha\left(\nabla_W L_{\text{data}} + \frac{\lambda}{N}W\right) = (1 - \frac{\alpha\lambda}{N})W - \alpha\nabla_W L_{\text{data}}$$

See that $$(1 - \frac{\alpha\lambda}{N})W$$ factor? At each step, the weights are multiplied by a number slightly less than 1 — they "decay" toward zero. This is why L2 regularization is also called weight decay.

AdamW distinction
With vanilla Adam, L2 regularization and weight decay are not the same thing. Adam's adaptive learning rate divides the gradient by $$\sqrt{v}$$, which also divides the regularization gradient — distorting the intended decay. AdamW fixes this by decoupling weight decay from the gradient update, applying the decay directly to the weights rather than through the gradient. This is why AdamW is the standard optimizer for modern deep learning.
Intuition
Why only weights, not biases? Weights control the complexity of the function the network learns — large weights create steep, sharp decision boundaries. Biases just shift the function up or down without adding complexity. Regularizing biases would prevent the network from properly centering its predictions, which hurts more than it helps.

07 — ReferenceThe Complete Picture

Every gradient we've derived, in one place. These are the building blocks you chain together to backpropagate through any neural network.

Component Gradient
Sigmoid + BCE $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = \hat{y} - y$$
Softmax + CE $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \hat{y}_k - y_k$$
BatchNorm (input) $$\frac{1}{N\sqrt{\sigma^2+\epsilon}}\left(N d\hat{x}_i - \Sigma d\hat{x} - \hat{x}_i \Sigma d\hat{x}\hat{x}\right)$$
Dropout $$\frac{m_i}{1-p} \cdot \text{upstream gradient}$$
L2 Regularization $$\nabla_W L_{\text{data}} + \frac{\lambda}{N}W$$

Notice a pattern: the most mathematically complex derivations (sigmoid+BCE, softmax+CE) produce the simplest final gradients. The simplest concepts (dropout, L2) have the most transparent backward passes. And BatchNorm sits in between — moderately complex derivation, moderately complex result — which is exactly why it's the one that shows up as "derive this" in interviews.

If you remember one thing from this article, let it be this: backpropagation is not one algorithm. It's a framework — the chain rule applied systematically — and what matters is knowing the derivative of each individual piece. Chain them together, and the gradients take care of themselves.
